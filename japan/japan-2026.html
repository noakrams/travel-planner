Silver Week starts the morning after you land

Japanese law turns any lone workday caught between two holidays into a holiday of its own. In 2026 that produces a five-day national break — the first since 2015, the next in 2032 — and it covers your entire first stretch.

Hotel rates run 40–57% above normal, and the Nozomi shinkansen drops its non-reserved cars entirely — no walk-up seats. The crowds are domestic, not foreign: Japanese families, going where you'd go. Both itineraries below solve this the same way, which is the way the Israeli Facebook groups solve it: don't move between cities during it. Sit in Tokyo, which absorbs crowds better than Kyoto or a mountain town ever could.

One exception, and it's deliberate. You leave Tokyo on Wed 23 Sep — the Equinox, the last day of the run, and the heaviest return-travel day of the five. This is the price of having two Tokyo nights at the end rather than one. It's payable: reserve your seat the minute the 30-day booking window opens, travel mid-morning rather than in the evening surge, and remember that your leg is outbound from Tokyo while most of the traffic is heading back into it.

The shape of the trip

Fourteen nights, starting and ending in Tokyo, with the mountain leg at Nikko — settled. Osaka is a day trip from Kyoto rather than its own base, which is the trade that makes everything else fit at a two-night minimum per stop.

The settled itinerary.
LegDatesNightsWhat
Tokyo 18–22 Sep 5 Land Fri evening; ride out most of Silver Week in the city
Nikko 23–24 Sep 2 Toshogu, then Oku-Nikko's lake, falls and the Senjogahara marsh trail
Kyoto 25–29 Sep 5 With Nara and Osaka as day trips
Tokyo 30 Sep – 1 Oct 2 Then a full last day before the 22:50 flight

Nikko is what you wanted, over the research's lean toward Hakone, and it earns it on sights: Toshogu is rated "Best of Japan" by japan-guide, and Oku-Nikko gives you a genuine 2.5–3 hour hike across the Senjogahara boardwalk at 1,400 m — the one real nature day on this trip, and none of it depends on the weather. The cost you're accepting: Nikko is a dead end two hours north of Tokyo, so the transfer to Kyoto on 25 Sep eats most of a day (backtrack through Tokyo, ~5 hours door to door).

You won't get autumn colour — don't let anyone tell you otherwise. Kyoto and Tokyo don't turn until the second half of November. Nikko's colour begins descending from Yumoto Onsen in early October and peaks around Lake Chuzenji mid-to-late October — after you've flown home. Expect green.

Don't buy the JR Pass

It is the most common expensive mistake on this exact route, and the arithmetic isn't close. The nationwide pass rose from about ¥29,650 to ¥50,000 in October 2023 and has never made sense for a Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka loop since.

Point-to-point fares, per person, reserved seats.

Tokyo → KyotoHikari shinkansen¥13,850
Kyoto → Osaka (day trip, return)JR Kyoto line — no shinkansen¥1,160

Total, point to point≈ ¥31,170
7-day nationwide JR Pass¥50,000
A 7-day pass would technically cover both long legs — and still loses by about ¥18,800. Started on 25 Sep it runs through 1 Oct, catching Tokyo→Kyoto, the Nara and Osaka day trips, and Kyoto→Tokyo. It covers roughly ¥31,000 of travel for ¥50,000. It also excludes the Nozomi, so you'd be on slower Hikari trains both ways, and it's dead weight on Tokyo's subways and the Tobu line to Nikko — the networks you actually use most. Buy individual tickets; tap an IC card for everything local.

What you should buy for the mountain leg: the Tobu Nikko All Area Pass (¥8,000 / 4 days) reaches Chuzenji and Yumoto; the cheaper ¥3,000 City Area pass does not go up the mountain, which is where you're spending the day.

Tokyo · 7 nights, both ends

You wanted somewhere alive at night and on a rail hub, not the dead centre. That points at Ueno — a Yamanote hub where the Skyliner from Narita terminates, with Ameyoko's izakaya alleys open late and a two-stop hop to Asakusa (where the Nikko train leaves). The Israeli groups compared Ueno directly with Ikebukuro and preferred it: "we enjoyed Ueno much more… a huge stunning park, and an area of bars and izakaya that's really fun to end the day in." Booking the same hotel for both Tokyo blocks also means free luggage storage while you're in Kyoto.

Things that will otherwise catch you out

Suica top-ups reject Visa

Foreign-issued Visa cards cannot top up a digital Suica in Apple Wallet — a network-level block, not a bug. Use Mastercard or Amex, or load a physical card with cash. Tapping a Visa to pay a shop works fine.

Oversized bags need a free reservation

On the Tokaido shinkansen, luggage over 160 cm total (height + width + depth) requires a free reserved slot. Turn up without one and it's ¥1,000. Most check-in cases are under. Forwarding your bags sidesteps this entirely.

Typhoon season is still open

September is the statistical peak. Trains get "planned suspensions" announced a day ahead, and JR refunds them in full. It usually costs you one day, not the trip. Avoid non-refundable rates for the first half.

Tattoos and onsen

You're naked in an onsen, so clothing doesn't cover them, and many communal baths refuse tattooed guests outright. The reliable answer is a ryokan room with a private open-air bath — always permitted, and you bathe together. Both Euro hotels in Nikko have one in every room, which is exactly why they're the pick for your mountain leg.

Museums close Mondays — except this one

Mon 21 Sep is a public holiday, so museums open and shift their closure to Tue 22 Sep instead. Mon 28 Sep, a Kyoto day, is a normal Monday and they'll be shut.

It will be hot

Late September is the tail of summer, not autumn: Tokyo and Osaka around 27–29°C and humid. It breaks in the first days of October. Nikko at 1,400m is the exception — single-digit nights.
