Silver Week starts the morning after you land

Japanese law turns any lone workday caught between two holidays into a holiday of its own. In 2026 that produces a five-day national break — the first since 2015, the next in 2032 — and it covers your entire first stretch.

Hotel rates run 40–57% above normal, and the Nozomi shinkansen drops its non-reserved cars entirely — no walk-up seats. The crowds are domestic, not foreign: Japanese families, going where you'd go. Both itineraries below solve this the same way, which is the way the Israeli Facebook groups solve it: don't move between cities during it. Sit in Tokyo, which absorbs crowds better than Kyoto or a mountain town ever could.

One exception, and it's deliberate. You leave Tokyo on Wed 23 Sep — the Equinox, the last day of the run, and the heaviest return-travel day of the five. This is the price of having two Tokyo nights at the end rather than one. It's payable: reserve your seat the minute the 30-day booking window opens, travel mid-morning rather than in the evening surge, and remember that your leg is outbound from Tokyo while most of the traffic is heading back into it.

The shape of the trip

Fourteen nights, starting and ending in Tokyo. Nikko is trimmed to a single night — Toshogu and out. Kyoto holds four nights as the temple base; Osaka now gets two nights of its own, reached on a Nara-and-Uji travelling day, before the run back east to Tokyo.

Nikko is what you wanted, over the research's lean toward Hakone, and on a single night it comes down to one thing: Toshogu, rated "Best of Japan" by japan-guide. That's the trade you've chosen by dropping the second night — the Oku-Nikko hiking day is gone (Senjogahara boardwalk, Kegon Falls, Yumoto's sulfur baths), and with it the trip's one real nature day. What's left is a tight, high-value visit: arrive, see the shrine, sleep in town, and ride down to Kyoto the next morning. The other cost stands — Nikko is a dead end two hours north of Tokyo, so the transfer to Kyoto on 24 Sep still eats most of a day (backtrack through Tokyo, ~5 hours door to door).

You won't get autumn colour — don't let anyone tell you otherwise. Kyoto and Tokyo don't turn until the second half of November. Nikko's colour begins descending from Yumoto Onsen in early October and peaks around Lake Chuzenji mid-to-late October — after you've flown home. Expect green.

What to book, and when

Ordered by urgency. Two of these have already closed for your dates — better to know now than to discover it in September.

The Nikko room · 23–24 Sept · 1 night
Still the scarcest booking of the trip — 23 Sept is a public holiday, and the good private-bath rooms are 12-to-16-room inns that sell out on that night first, so trimming to one night doesn't make it any easier to get. Aim for Hotel Casual Euro or Hotel Euro City (private open-air bath in every room; book the breakfast-only plan so you dine in town), on Rakuten Travel — Booking.com wrongly shows Casual Euro as full for these dates while Rakuten has it.

Don't buy the JR Pass

It is the most common expensive mistake on this exact route, and the arithmetic isn't close. The nationwide pass rose from about ¥29,650 to ¥50,000 in October 2023 and has never made sense for a Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka loop since.

A 7-day pass would technically cover both long shinkansen legs — and still loses by about ¥21,000. Started on 24 Sep it runs through 30 Sep, catching Tokyo→Kyoto, the JR Nara-line hops on the 28th, and Shin-Osaka→Tokyo. It doesn't even cover the Kintetsu run from Nara into Osaka — that's not a JR line — and it excludes the Nozomi, so you'd be on slower Hikari trains. It's dead weight on Tokyo's subways and the Tobu line to Nikko too, the networks you actually use most. Buy individual tickets; tap an IC card for everything local.

What you should buy for the mountain leg: the Tobu Nikko All Area Pass (¥8,000 / 4 days) reaches Chuzenji and Yumoto; the cheaper ¥3,000 City Area pass does not go up the mountain, which is where you're spending the day.

Tokyo · Part 1 (5 nights, 18–22 Sept)

Re-researched on 11 July across Booking, the Facebook groups and the hotels' own sites, filtered hard: every option below is a private room with its own private bathroom (nothing shared), close to a station you can commute from, and — bar the two clearly-labelled splurges — under ₪1,000/night. Walking time to the nearest useful station is on every card. Shibuya is your focus, so it leads; Asakusa still wins overall because the Nikko train leaves from its doorstep.

Nikko · 1 night (23–24 Sept)

The tattoos and the diet decide this leg. A traditional ryokan serves a fixed, fish-heavy kaiseki (no chicken option) and its onsen is communal (tattoos refused). Both problems vanish at the two Euro sister hotels: a private open-air bath in every room, and a breakfast-only booking so you eat dinner in town. Sleep in Nikko town, not Yumoto — Yumoto has no restaurants and no konbini, which would lock you into the ryokan dinner. On one night, being walkable to dinner matters more than ever.

Things that will otherwise catch you out

Suica top-ups reject Visa

Foreign-issued Visa cards cannot top up a digital Suica in Apple Wallet — a network-level block, not a bug. Use Mastercard or Amex, or load a physical card with cash. Tapping a Visa to pay a shop works fine.

Oversized bags need a free reservation

On the Tokaido shinkansen, luggage over 160 cm total (height + width + depth) requires a free reserved slot. Turn up without one and it's ¥1,000. Most check-in cases are under. Forwarding your bags sidesteps this entirely.

Typhoon season is still open

September is the statistical peak. Trains get "planned suspensions" announced a day ahead, and JR refunds them in full. It usually costs you one day, not the trip. Avoid non-refundable rates for the first half.

Tattoos and onsen

You're naked in an onsen, so clothing doesn't cover them, and many communal baths refuse tattooed guests outright. The reliable answer is a ryokan room with a private open-air bath — always permitted, and you bathe together. Both Euro hotels in Nikko have one in every room, which is exactly why they're the pick for your mountain leg.

Museums close Mondays — except this one

Mon 21 Sep is a public holiday, so museums open and shift their closure to Tue 22 Sep instead. Mon 28 Sep is your Nara + Uji travelling day — no matter, as Tōdai-ji, Byōdō-in and the shrines don't take a Monday closure the way city museums do.

It will be hot

Late September is the tail of summer, not autumn: Tokyo and Osaka around 27–29°C and humid. It breaks in the first days of October. Nikko at 1,400m is the exception — single-digit nights.
